Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

Chemistry Behind It

Uses nuclear magnetic resonance of hydrogen atoms in water molecules.

A strong magnetic field aligns the spins of hydrogen protons, and radio waves excite them.

Different tissues emit distinct signals based on their water content and relaxation times.

Applications

  • Medical imaging of soft tissues, including brain scans, tumor detection, and joint examinations
  • Research tool for studying brain function and neurological disorders
  • Veterinary medicine for diagnosing animal health issues

Benefits

  • Medical: Non-invasive imaging of soft tissues
  • Economic: Multi-billion dollar industry
  • Social: Improved diagnosis without radiation

Safety Considerations

  • Strong magnetic fields require non-magnetic environments
  • Not suitable for patients with metal implants or pacemakers
  • Requires trained operators and specialized equipment
